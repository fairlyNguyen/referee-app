Iran calls for human chains to protect power plants as Trump's deadline nears

By Bassem Mroue, Jon Gambrell, and Samy Magdy, Associated Press
April 7, 2026, 7:07 a.m. ET · Updated April 7, 2026, 11:02 p.m. ET
Bystanders watch from a distance as rescue teams and first responders work at the site of a strike. Francisco Seco / AP

BEIRUT — Iran on Monday called on citizens to form human chains around nuclear and power installations to deter possible U.S. or Israeli military strikes, as Washington's latest deadline for a nuclear deal drew closer without a resolution.

Iran's Supreme National Security Council said the country had agreed to a ceasefire and would negotiate with the United States in Islamabad beginning Friday, but tensions remained high over Tehran's nuclear program.

Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei addressed the nation in a rare public statement, urging Iranians to stand firm against foreign pressure and warning that any military action against Iranian territory would be met with a "decisive response."

The call for human shields came as satellite imagery showed increased activity at several Iranian nuclear sites, including Fordow and Natanz, raising fears of an imminent escalation. U.S. officials said negotiations were ongoing but described the situation as "extremely fragile."

President Trump issued a statement from Mar-a-Lago late Sunday reiterating that Iran "cannot be allowed" to obtain a nuclear weapon, and that all options remained on the table. His remarks followed a series of back-channel talks between American and Iranian diplomats in Oman last week.

In Tehran, thousands gathered in public squares following the Supreme Leader's broadcast, with many chanting slogans of support outside the Bushehr nuclear power plant — Iran's only operational civilian reactor. Similar scenes were reported in Mashhad and Isfahan.

European allies expressed alarm at the developments, with France, Germany, and the United Kingdom issuing a joint statement calling for "maximum diplomatic restraint" and urging all parties to return to the negotiating table. The European Union's foreign policy chief called for an emergency meeting of the U.N. Security Council.

Israel, which has repeatedly warned it will not allow Iran to acquire nuclear weapons, did not comment publicly on the latest developments. Israeli media, however, cited unnamed defense officials as saying the military was "monitoring the situation closely."

The human chain initiative, organized by the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps and promoted via state television, is seen by analysts as both a propaganda move and a genuine attempt to complicate targeting decisions for any potential strike. Civilian presence around military and nuclear sites would raise the diplomatic and moral cost of any attack.

"This is a classic use of human shields, dressed up as civil defense," said Ariane Tabatabai, a senior fellow at the German Marshall Fund who focuses on Iranian nuclear policy. "It puts any attacking force in an impossible position, and that's exactly the point."

Iran's foreign minister, Abbas Araghchi, met with the Pakistani foreign minister in Islamabad on Monday ahead of the scheduled talks, signaling that Tehran was still open to diplomacy even as it mobilized citizens domestically. He reiterated Iran's position that it would not negotiate under military threat.

The Trump administration has set a series of escalating deadlines for Iran to agree to a new nuclear framework, with the latest expiring at the end of the week. Senior officials have said the president is "seriously considering" a military option if diplomacy fails, but has not yet made a final decision.

Oil markets responded to the tension, with Brent crude rising more than 4% to its highest level in six months. Analysts warned that any military strike near Iranian energy infrastructure could send prices above $120 a barrel and trigger a broader regional conflict.

Congressional leaders offered mixed reactions, with Republican hawks backing a tougher stance and moderate Democrats urging the White House to exhaust all diplomatic channels. Senate Majority Leader John Thune said the administration had his "full support" in confronting Iranian nuclear ambitions.

The Pentagon declined to detail troop movements or force posture changes in the region, but officials confirmed that the aircraft carrier USS Harry S. Truman remains in the Eastern Mediterranean and that U.S. Air Force assets in Qatar and Bahrain have been placed on elevated readiness.

In Philadelphia, community members with ties to the Iranian diaspora held a candlelight vigil outside City Hall on Sunday evening, calling for peace and opposing military intervention. "Our families are there," said one attendee. "We are begging for diplomacy."
